# Attachment styles

How early caregivers shape adult relationship patterns. Useful for naming what's happening in romantic and close relationships without diagnosing.

## The four styles

- **Secure** — comfortable with intimacy and independence. Trusts that needs will be met. Can repair after conflict. About 50% of people.
- **Anxious (preoccupied)** — craves closeness, fears abandonment, hyper-vigilant to signs of withdrawal, protests when partner pulls away (more texts, more calls, escalation). Often paired with avoidants in painful loops.
- **Avoidant (dismissive)** — values independence, uncomfortable with too much closeness, deactivates when intimacy intensifies (pulls away, goes quiet, finds flaws). Often genuinely doesn't notice they're doing it.
- **Disorganized (fearful-avoidant)** — wants closeness AND fears it. Push-pull. Often rooted in caregivers who were both source of comfort and source of fear. Most volatile, most likely to feel "crazy" in love.

## The anxious-avoidant trap

The most common painful dynamic. Anxious protests, avoidant withdraws, anxious escalates, avoidant withdraws harder. Both feel justified. Both feel unloved. Neither is "the bad one" — the dance is the problem.

## Earned secure

Attachment isn't a fixed sentence. With awareness, therapy, secure relationships, regulation work, people move toward secure over time.

## How to use this in voice

- "Bestie, you're not crazy for needing reassurance. You've got an anxious attachment and he's pulling avoidant moves. Your nervous system is doing exactly what it's wired to do."
- "Sis, the more you chase, the more he runs. That's the dance. The only way out is to stop dancing."
- "He's not 'bad at love', babe, he's avoidant. Which doesn't excuse what he did. But it explains the pattern."

Don't diagnose the absent third party. Speculate carefully, label patterns not people.

## When attachment is NOT the answer

If someone is abusive, manipulative, or cruel, "they're just avoidant" is not an explanation. Don't let attachment theory become apologism for harm.
