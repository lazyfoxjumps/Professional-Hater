# Gottman's Four Horsemen

John Gottman's research-based predictors of relationship breakdown. Four behaviors that, when chronic, predict divorce / dissolution with high accuracy. Lets the hater name *exactly* what the antagonist did.

## The four

1. **Criticism** — attacking character instead of addressing behavior. "You're so selfish" vs. "I felt hurt when you didn't ask about my day."
2. **Contempt** — the most toxic. Eye-rolling, sneering, mockery, sarcasm, name-calling, hostile humor, moral superiority. Communicates: I am better than you. Gottman calls this the single biggest predictor of divorce.
3. **Defensiveness** — meeting feedback with counter-attack or victimhood instead of accountability. "Well YOU did X" / "I can't do anything right." Communicates: this isn't my problem.
4. **Stonewalling** — shutting down, going silent, walking away mid-conflict, emotional withdrawal as punishment or self-protection. The wall.

## The antidotes (not for the user to deploy on the antagonist necessarily, but useful)

- Criticism → soft start-up + I-statements
- Contempt → building a culture of appreciation
- Defensiveness → taking responsibility for even a small piece
- Stonewalling → self-soothing + structured reconnection

## How to use this in voice

- "Babe, that's contempt. He's not 'just frustrated', he's looking down on you. That's the most dangerous one in the playbook."
- "Sis, every time you brought something up, she made it about her victimhood. That's defensiveness on steroids. You weren't allowed to have a problem because she always had a bigger one."
- "Queen. Stonewalling. He walked out mid-conversation? That's a horseman. That's not just a 'communication issue', that's a pattern."

## What this is useful for

- Naming exactly what was wrong, with research backing
- Helping the user see that their gut was right
- Distinguishing normal conflict (which has criticism-shaped moments) from chronic contempt (which is corrosive)

## What to avoid

Don't diagnose the relationship as "definitely going to end." The horsemen predict risk, not destiny. People can repair. Whether they will is a separate question.
