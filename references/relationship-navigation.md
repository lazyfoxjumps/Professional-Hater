# Relationship navigation

Romantic relationships, situationships, and the in-between. Big-sister knowledge bank.

## Repair after a fight

Most fights aren't about the thing. They're about the meaning. He didn't text back → real issue: "I don't feel prioritized." She made a joke → real issue: "I don't feel respected in front of your friends."

Repair sequence:
1. **Cool down first.** Don't repair while dysregulated. 20-90 minutes minimum if hyperaroused.
2. **Lead with the meaning, not the incident.** "When you canceled, I felt like I wasn't a priority. Not 'you canceled and you're a bad person.'"
3. **Take a small piece of accountability** even when you're 80% in the right. ("I could've brought this up sooner instead of stewing.")
4. **Ask for the specific thing you need going forward.** Behavior change, not feelings change.
5. **Repair physically if appropriate.** Touch, sit close, soften the body. Words alone often don't land.

If they refuse to repair after a few attempts, that's data. Healthy people repair. The unwillingness IS the problem.

## "Where is this going" talk

When to have it: when not knowing is costing you more than the answer might.

How:
- Don't open with "we need to talk" (puts them on defense).
- Open with what YOU want: "I've been thinking about what I want out of this. I want [X]. I wanted to check in about whether we're heading the same direction."
- Listen to the answer. The first response is usually the truer one. Watered-down clarifications afterward are often face-saving.
- A "I don't know" after six months is a no. People who want you, claim you.
- An "I need more time" without a clear what-for is usually a no with extra steps.

## Breakups, three types

**Mutual.** Rare. Honor it. Don't relitigate. The hater helps mourn what was real.

**You're leaving him.** Be clear, be kind, be brief. Don't list grievances; you're not in court. "This isn't working for me anymore. I've thought about it. I'm not going to change my mind." Don't stay to comfort. That's not your job anymore.

**He's leaving you (blindsided).** The hardest. The grief is doubled because you didn't get to brace. See [grief-stages.md](grief-stages.md). The big-sister truth: people who leave like that usually had one foot out for longer than you knew. The blindsiding is information about them, not a referendum on you.

**Slow-fade breakup.** When neither of you said the words but the relationship is dying. Don't let it. Name it. "I think we're in a slow fade. Are we?" Direct ends are kinder than dragged-out ambiguity.

## Jealousy work

Jealousy is a signal, not a verdict. It's pointing at something. Often:
- A real boundary being crossed (valid).
- Past trauma being reactivated (yours to work on, but partner can help).
- Your own insecurities about not being enough (yours to work on).
- Comparison fed by social media (close the app).

The mature move is: feel the jealousy, locate where it's coming from, decide whether it's data or noise. Don't act on it from the dysregulated state.

## Dating with attachment awareness

If you're anxious-attached: dating avoidants will feel like love-bombing → withdrawal cycles. The intensity isn't depth. Look for consistency.

If you're avoidant-attached: secure people will feel "boring" because there's no chase. Boring is what safety feels like before your nervous system recalibrates. Don't run.

Look for: someone who repairs after conflict, follows through on small things, can talk about feelings without panicking, and treats your needs as information rather than burdens.

Walk from: love-bombing in the first month, future-faking before they've earned trust, hot/cold patterns, anyone who makes you feel "crazy" for normal asks.

## When to leave vs. when to stay

**Stay if:** the bad days are bad but the good days are real, you're both showing up to repair, the pattern is improving, your nervous system is mostly safe with them.

**Leave if:** you've named the same problem repeatedly and nothing changes, you can't be yourself around them, you feel smaller than you did a year ago, your friends are quietly worried, you're scared of them (in any sense), the cost of staying is exceeding what staying gives back.

**Get out NOW if:** physical, sexual, or financial abuse; coercive control; threats; isolation tactics. This is safety territory, not relationship-navigation territory.

## How to use this in voice

- "Sis, real talk. He's not 'just bad at communicating.' He's communicating perfectly. The message is 'I don't want this enough to try.' That's clear."
- "Babe, where you are right now? I've been there. I stayed three years longer than I should have because the good days were really good. Don't do what I did. Be honest about the math."
- "Queen, listen. You're allowed to leave a relationship that isn't abusive. You don't need a justification. 'It's not working for me' is enough."
