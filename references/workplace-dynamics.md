# Workplace dynamics

How to navigate work without absorbing the toxicity. Big-sister knowledge bank for managing up, dealing with difficult coworkers, pushing back, escalating, and knowing when to leave.

## Managing up

Your manager has their own boss, their own stress, their own gaps. Managing up means making it easy for them to give you what you need.

- **Send weekly updates** even if not asked. Three bullets: what's done, what's next, what's blocked. Builds trust before you need it.
- **Bring solutions, not problems.** "Here's the issue, here are two options, here's what I'd recommend." Beats dumping a problem on their desk.
- **Ask explicitly for what you want.** Raises, promotions, projects, time off. Most managers won't volunteer; they'll respond if asked.
- **Document everything.** Especially feedback. Especially promises ("we'll revisit your title in Q3"). Especially decisions made in 1:1s. Write it back to them in a follow-up message so you have it.

## Credit theft

The coworker who takes credit for your work. Common, infuriating, fixable.

Tactics:
- **Pre-claim.** In meetings, say "the version I built does X" before they can.
- **Paper trail.** Send the email with the proposal. Now it's timestamped to you.
- **Don't fight them in public.** Bring it to your manager privately with receipts. "I noticed [X]. I want to make sure my contributions are visible. Here's the documentation."
- **Loop in upward.** When the work is yours, cc the manager. Now they see who's doing it.

If your manager keeps siding with the credit thief: that's data about your manager, not just the thief.

## Pushback without burning bridges

You can disagree at work without making it war. Formula:

1. **Acknowledge what's right** in their position. "I see why you'd want to go that direction; the simplicity is real."
2. **Surface the concern as a question, not an accusation.** "Have we thought about how it'll handle [edge case]?"
3. **Offer an alternative.** "What if we did [X] instead?"
4. **Defer if outranked, but get it in writing.** "Got it, let's go your way. Just so I'm clear, the call is [X] and we own the risk on [Y]."

Last step is the secret weapon. Documents the decision so when [Y] blows up, the record is clear.

## Escalate / document / leave decision tree

When a coworker, manager, or culture is harming you, three options. Pick by severity and the company's actual integrity.

- **Document only:** mild but repeated issues. Quietly build a paper trail. Don't escalate yet; you might leave first.
- **Escalate (HR, skip-level, ethics line):** clear violations, patterns of harm, anything illegal. Go in with documentation, dates, specifics. Expect HR to protect the company first; that's their job. Have your own backup (lawyer, allies, exit plan) before you escalate.
- **Leave:** when the cost of staying exceeds what staying gives back, AND the situation isn't going to improve from inside. Sometimes the best move isn't to fix the workplace, it's to find a different one.

## Bad-manager survival

If your manager is the problem:
- **Identify which kind.** Incompetent (over-promoted, doesn't know what they're doing) vs. malicious (taking credit, sabotaging, gaslighting) vs. absent (you're managing yourself). Each has different tactics.
- **Document interactions.** Times, content, witnesses.
- **Build relationships above and around.** Skip-level meetings, cross-functional partners. So your reputation isn't held only by one person.
- **Stop emotionally investing.** You can do good work without giving them your spirit. Save the spirit for outside the building.
- **Plan an exit timeline** even if you don't act on it. Knowing you have an option changes the felt sense.

## Salary negotiation basics

- **Always counter.** Almost any offer has flex.
- **Use the line:** "Thank you for the offer. Based on my research and the value I bring, I was hoping for [X]. Is there room to move?"
- **Negotiate the whole package, not just base.** Equity, bonus, signing bonus, PTO, remote flexibility, title, start date.
- **Don't share your current salary** if you can avoid it. In many places, illegal to ask. Anchor on market rate, not history.
- **Silence is a tool.** After you state your counter, stop talking. Let them respond.

## Toxic culture without absorbing it

Some workplaces are sick. You can survive while you plan exit:
- **Compartmentalize.** Don't take it home. Don't make it your identity.
- **Don't try to fix the unfixable.** Save energy for what you control.
- **Stay close to allies.** One trusted coworker is a lifeline.
- **Limit overtime.** Toxic cultures love overgivers. Match input to what they're actually paying for.
- **Document everything** in case you need it later.

## When to quit

Quit if:
- Health (mental or physical) is being affected.
- You've stopped learning AND there's no path forward.
- The values mismatch is fundamental (you can't ethically do what they're asking).
- Compensation is consistently below market and they won't move.
- A new opportunity is genuinely better, not just shinier.

Don't quit (yet) if:
- You'd be leaving in a flash of emotion without an exit plan.
- The grass-is-greener feeling is generic, not specific.
- You haven't actually asked for what you want.
- A specific problem is solvable and you haven't tried solving it.

**Never quit without a plan when you can avoid it.** Quit-and-figure-it-out works for some people; for most, it makes the next move harder.

## How to use this in voice

- "Sis, listen. He's stealing your work. We're not having a feelings conversation with him about it. We're sending an email, cc'ing your manager, with the version YOU built attached. Receipts. Quietly. Like a professional."
- "Babe, real talk: HR is not your friend. HR is the company's friend. By all means escalate, but go in with documentation and an exit plan, not vibes."
- "Queen, between us. You've been counting down to Friday since Monday for eight months. That's not 'work is hard.' That's 'this job is taking from you faster than it's giving.' Let's plan the exit."
