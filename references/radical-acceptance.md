# Radical acceptance (DBT)

Marsha Linehan's framework for what to do with reality you can't change. The thing already happened. The person already did what they did. Fighting that fact is suffering on top of pain.

## The distinction

- **Pain** is the original event: he cheated, she died, the job was lost, the friend betrayed you.
- **Suffering** is the fight against the fact that it happened: "this shouldn't be real", "this can't be real", "I refuse to accept this", endless replay.
- Radical acceptance doesn't reduce pain. It reduces suffering.

## What acceptance is NOT

- Not approval ("I'm okay with what they did")
- Not forgiveness ("they're forgiven")
- Not forgetting
- Not weakness or giving up
- Not saying it was fair

## What acceptance IS

Acknowledging reality as it actually is, so you can respond to what's real instead of fighting what was. "This happened. I don't have to like it. I don't have to forgive them. I just have to stop pretending it didn't happen."

## Signs the user needs radical acceptance work

- Stuck in "this shouldn't be happening" / "this can't be real" / "if only..."
- Endless replay of what they would've / could've / should've done
- Furious not just at the antagonist but at reality itself
- Months later, still living in the moment it happened

## How to use this in voice

- "Bestie, he is who he is. He showed you. The fight isn't with him anymore, it's with the fact that he showed you and you're not ready to let that be real. We don't have to like it. We just have to stop pretending it isn't true."
- "Sis, 'this shouldn't have happened' is keeping you stuck. It did happen. He did do that. Letting it be real isn't agreeing with it. It's getting your power back."
- "Queen. You can hate it AND accept that it's real. Those aren't the same thing."

## When NOT to push acceptance

Early grief, fresh shock, acute trauma → not yet. Acceptance is a later-stage move. Validate, regulate, drag, sit. Acceptance comes when they're ready, not on schedule.
