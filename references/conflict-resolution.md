# Conflict resolution

How to actually fight well, repair after, apologize properly, receive apologies, and know the difference between conflict and incompatibility.

## Soft start-up vs. hard start-up (Gottman)

How a conversation begins predicts how it ends with 96% accuracy in Gottman's research. The first 3 minutes matter the most.

**Hard start-up:** criticism, sarcasm, contempt right out of the gate. "You always do this." "Why can't you just..." The other person goes defensive, the conversation is dead.

**Soft start-up:**
1. Lead with "I", not "you."
2. Describe the situation neutrally.
3. Express a feeling.
4. Make a specific request.

Example:
- Hard: "You never help with anything around here."
- Soft: "Hey, when the kitchen is left like this after I cooked, I feel taken for granted. Can we agree on a cleanup rhythm?"

## Repair attempts

Repair attempts are the small bids during or after conflict that say "I want us to be okay." Humor, touch, "I love you," "let me try that again," eye contact, an apology mid-fight.

Successful couples don't fight less. They REPAIR more. The willingness to soften mid-conflict, even when you're still mad, is the marker.

Failure mode: rejecting all repair attempts because you're "still upset." Then they stop trying. Then resentment sets in.

## How to apologize properly (Harriet Lerner-style)

Most apologies fail because they include one or more of these:
- "I'm sorry BUT..." (cancels the apology)
- "I'm sorry IF I hurt you..." (conditionalizes their experience)
- "I'm sorry you FEEL that way..." (blames their feelings, not your behavior)
- "I'm sorry, ARE WE GOOD?" (transfers your discomfort to them)

A real apology has four parts:
1. **Name what you did.** Specifically. Without minimizing.
2. **Acknowledge the impact.** Their experience, not your intent.
3. **Don't explain.** Excuses dilute. Save context for later if needed.
4. **Commit to specific change.** What will you do differently.

Example: "I dismissed you when you brought up [X]. I made you feel like your concern didn't matter. I'm not going to do that again. Next time you bring something up, I'll listen first and respond after."

The other person doesn't owe you forgiveness on your timeline.

## How to RECEIVE an apology

Often skipped. Receiving an apology badly kills the repair.

If it's a good apology: acknowledge it. "Thank you. That means something to me. I'm not over it yet, but this helps."

If it's a fake apology: name what's missing. "I appreciate you saying sorry. But I don't think you've actually acknowledged what happened. Can we try again?"

You don't have to forgive in the moment. "I hear you. I need some time to sit with this." is complete.

## Conflict vs. incompatibility

Some conflicts are about something fixable. Some are about a fundamental mismatch.

**Conflict (workable):**
- Different communication styles (can be bridged).
- Different needs in specific moments (can be negotiated).
- Misunderstandings (can be clarified).
- Different defaults that no one's articulated.

**Incompatibility (often not workable through "trying harder"):**
- Different core values.
- Different visions for the future (kids, marriage, geography).
- Different needs for closeness/independence in a way that doesn't budge.
- Different willingness to do the work.

Telling these apart matters. Couples therapy can do real work on conflict; it can't bend incompatibility into compatibility.

## When to walk away mid-conflict

If contempt, name-calling, threats, or stonewalling enter the conversation, the conversation is over. Productive talk is no longer possible in that state.

How: "I'm not in a place to keep talking right now. I'm going to step away. Let's come back to this in [time]."

Then actually come back. Walking away that turns into stonewalling is its own problem.

## The four conflict styles

- **Avoiders** — wait for things to blow over, conflict-averse, may resent silently. Need to learn to bring things up.
- **Validators** — express, listen, repair. Healthiest style.
- **Volatiles** — passionate, expressive, fight hard. Can be healthy if respect is intact.
- **Hostile-distant** — contempt without engagement. Highest risk.

You and your partner / friend / family member don't have to share a style. But hostile-distant is the one that doesn't work no matter what the other person does.

## How to use this in voice

- "Sis, real talk. That wasn't an apology. That was 'I'm sorry you're upset.' Different thing. Don't accept the bad apology just because you're tired of fighting."
- "Babe, I'm watching you do hard-start-up on him every time and then wonder why he goes defensive. We're going to redo this one. Lead with what you feel, not what he did."
- "Queen, between us, this isn't conflict. This is incompatibility. You've named the same thing twelve times. He's heard you. He's not going to change because the thing you want changed is who he is."
