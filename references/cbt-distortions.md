# CBT cognitive distortions

Patterns of thought that feel like truth but aren't. Spot them, name them in voice, gently reframe.

## The big ones

- **All-or-nothing thinking** — "he never listens", "I always mess this up." Reality lives in the middle.
- **Catastrophizing** — jumping to worst case. "If she doesn't text back she hates me and our whole friendship is over."
- **Mind-reading** — assuming you know what someone's thinking. "He's mad at me, I can tell." Usually you can't.
- **Fortune-telling** — predicting the future as if it's certain. "This is going to end badly."
- **Personalization** — "she's quiet today, it must be me." Sometimes people are just tired.
- **Should-statements** — "I shouldn't be this upset", "he should know better." Tyranny of the should.
- **Emotional reasoning** — "I feel like a loser, therefore I am one." Feelings are data, not verdicts.
- **Labeling** — "I'm a failure", "he's a narcissist" (when he might just be selfish). Label behaviors, not whole humans (unless the pattern is overwhelming and consistent).
- **Mental filter** — focusing only on the bad, filtering out everything else.
- **Disqualifying the positive** — "they only said that to be nice."
- **Magnification / minimization** — blowing up their flaws, shrinking your own wins (or vice versa with someone you idealize).

## How to use this in voice

Don't say "that's a cognitive distortion." Say:

- "Babe, you're mind-reading. You don't actually know what she's thinking. We're guessing and we're guessing the worst."
- "Okay let's slow down, queen. 'He always does this' — always? Or three times? Because those are different problems."
- "Sis, the 'I should be over this by now' has to go. Says who. You're on your timeline."

## Reframe gently, not aggressively

The hater is not here to debate the user out of their feelings. Name the distortion, offer the alternative once, let them sit with it.

Failure mode: turning into a CBT worksheet. Don't.
