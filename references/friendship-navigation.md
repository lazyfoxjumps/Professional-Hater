# Friendship navigation

Adult friendships are harder than they should be. Big-sister knowledge bank for slow fades, direct conversations, asymmetric dynamics, friend breakups, and making new friends past school.

## Slow fade vs. direct conversation

When a friendship is dying, two options.

**Slow fade.** Reduce contact, take longer to respond, stop initiating, let it drift. Works when:
- The friendship was casual and never deep.
- There's no rupture, just incompatibility revealed by time.
- Direct conversation would create more drama than the friendship is worth.
- You're both probably feeling the drift; nobody needs to make it a Thing.

**Direct conversation.** Sit down (or call) and say it. Works when:
- The friendship has been meaningful and deserves a real ending.
- There's a specific rupture that's poisoning the dynamic.
- The slow fade would hurt them more than honesty.
- You want closure or to attempt repair.

How to have the direct one:
- "Hey, I've been thinking about us. I want to be honest with you."
- Name what changed. Don't list grievances; describe the shift.
- Decide ahead of time whether you're trying to repair or to close. Different conversations.
- Don't deliver this over text if it can be voice or in-person.

## Asymmetric friendships

You always reach out. You always go to them. They never ask how you are. They show up when they need you.

Test: stop initiating for two weeks. Do they notice? If they don't, you have your answer.

Two paths:
1. **Have the conversation.** "I've noticed I'm the one initiating most of the time. I'd love to hear from you too sometimes. Is something off, or is this just where we are?"
2. **Accept the asymmetry on lower terms.** Some friendships are real but never going to be balanced. Stop expecting equal reciprocity, reduce the energy you give, keep them at the level they can sustain.

What NOT to do: stew in resentment while continuing the same level of giving. That's a recipe for explosion or quiet bitterness.

## Friend breakups

They're allowed to exist. Sometimes a friendship has run its course, or the person has shown you who they are, or you've grown in different directions, or they did something you can't get past.

How:
- Often less explanation is kinder than more. "I don't think I have space for this friendship right now" is complete.
- Don't ghost without warning if the friendship was real. Even one closing message respects what was shared.
- Block on social if seeing their content hurts. It's not pettiness; it's recovery.
- Grieve. Friend breakups are real losses and society underplays them. See [grief-stages.md](grief-stages.md).

## Making adult friends

Hard. The structure is gone (no shared classrooms, dorms, after-school clubs). Tactics:

- **Show up to the same place repeatedly.** Same gym class, same coffee shop, same hobby group, same neighborhood event. Proximity + repetition is how acquaintances form. Most friendships start as low-pressure repeat exposure.
- **Be the one who initiates the second hangout.** Most adults will say yes to a specific invitation and never propose one themselves. Be the proposer. Specifically: "Coffee Saturday at 11 at [place]?" not "we should hang out sometime."
- **Move from group to one-on-one.** Group hangouts are how you screen for chemistry. Real friendship deepens one-on-one.
- **Let the first conversation be surfacey, the second be slightly less so, the third be real.** Don't dump trauma on a new friend in week one. Don't stay forever in small talk either.
- **Accept the awkwardness.** Adult friendship-making is awkward. The people willing to be slightly awkward together are the ones who make it.

## Friend-group politics

When you're in a group, dynamics get layered.

- **The triangulator.** The friend who tells you what other friends said about you, often distorted. Reduce information flow. "I'd rather hear that from her directly."
- **The missing stair.** The friend everyone knows is difficult/unsafe/messy but no one names. The group works around them. If you can name it, do — once. If the group won't, you may need to step back from the group, not just the friend.
- **The center.** The person who organizes everything. If the center burns out or leaves, groups often dissolve. Recognize the labor. Sometimes you're the center; decide whether you want to keep being.
- **The new pairing tension.** When two friends in a group get closer and you start feeling left out. Communicate before the resentment sets in.
- **The "everyone's invited except..." move.** If you're being subtly excluded, name it once. If it continues, you have your answer about the group.

## The missing-stair friend

Named after the unfixed broken step on the staircase that everyone learns to step over instead of fixing.

Signs:
- People warn newcomers about them in private.
- "That's just how they are."
- They've had multiple ruptures and the group keeps absorbing the cost.
- They never apologize for the same recurring behavior.

You don't have to be the one who fixes it. You DO get to decide whether you keep stepping over the stair. Sometimes the move is to stop attending events where the stair is. Sometimes it's leaving the group. Sometimes it's saying "I'm not okay with how [missing stair] treats [person], and I'm not going to keep pretending I am."

## How to use this in voice

- "Sis, real talk. You've initiated every single hangout for 8 months. She's not your friend right now, she's an acquaintance you're carrying. Stop initiating for two weeks and let's see what happens."
- "Babe, listen. Adult friendships are made by being the person who proposes the specific plan. 'Coffee at 11 Saturday at [place].' Be that person. Most people are dying for someone to organize the social life they wish they had."
- "Queen, this is a missing-stair situation. The group is stepping around her and asking you to step around her too. You don't have to. But you should know that naming it might cost you the group, not just her. Decide which one you're more okay losing."
